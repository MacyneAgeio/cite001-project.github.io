NOTABLE MOON WALKERS

Neil Alden Armstrong (1930-2012)

The first man to walk on the Moon. Boarded on the Apollo 11, Gemini 8 space mission as the Commanding officer. And on July 20, 1969, it was then he landed on the surface of the moon, claiming it as the first step of a historical event. Prior to embarking his career as an Astronaut, he was previously an engineer, naval aviator, test pilot. He later pursued his master's degree in Aerospace engineering and took his profession as a university professor in Cincinnati, Ohio.

Awards and Honors: Presidential Medal of Freedom, Collier Trophy, Congressional Space Medal of Honor, Goddard Memorial Trophy.

Edwin "Buzz" Aldrin (1930)

He was the second person to step foot on the moon. An american astronaut who boarded the same ship as Neil A. Armstrong, NASA's Apollo 11, Gemini 8 mission. He was a distinguishable figure in the U.S. Air Force, whereto he participated in the Korean War. He received his Ph.D. in Austronautics from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology.

Awards and Honors: Presidential Medal for Freedom, Collier Trophy, Goddard Memorial Trophy, the Harmon International Trophy, Air Medal, NASA Distinguished Service Medal, Distinguished Service Medal, Hubbard Medal, NASA Space Flight Medal, Distinguished Flying Cross.

Charles "Pete" Conrad (1930-1999)

He also took part in the Gemini program during 1965, where he flew as the pilot of the Gemini 5. He was an exceptional individual, known not only for his charismatic character, but also for his outstounding expertise in engineering and piloting. And in 1973, he served his duties as a commander of the Skylab 2 mission. Before leaving the Navy and space program since 1974.

Awards and Honors: Congressional Space Medal of Honor, NASA Exceptional Service Medal, Navy Astronaut Wings, Multiple Decorations and Commendations, NASA Distinguished Service Medal, Distinguished Flying Crosses.

Alan LaVern Bean (1932-2018)

An American astronaut, naval officer, and artist. He first served as a U.S. Navy test pilot before joining into NASA's Apollo 12 mission in 1969 as the lunar module pilot. Along with his Commander, Charles "Pete" Conrad, they conducted two moonwalks, returning with valuable data samples from their mission. After Apollo 12, he partook in the Skylab Program, in which he was the comanding officer of the Skylab 3 mission in 1973. Thereafter his retirement, he pursued the work of Art, depicting his interpretation and experiences in space.

Awards and Honors: NASA Distinguished Service Medal, Navy Astronaut Wings, Navy Distinguished Flying Cross, Posthumous Recognition.

Alan Bartlett Shepard (1923-1998)

The first american astronaut to enter space. In 1959, he was a candidate for Project Mercury, designed to deploy humans into space. And in 1961, he accomplished a momentous feat, commanding the Freedom 7 spacecraft on a suborbital flight that lasted for about fifteen minutes with a height of 116 miles above Earth's atmosphere. Later on, in 1971, he commanded the Apollo 14 mission, in which he played golf on the moon's surface.

Awards and Honors: Congressional Space Medal of Honor, NASA Exceptional Achievement Medal, NASA Distinguished Service Medal, Distinguised Flying Cross.

Edgar Dean Mitchell (1930-2016)

Joined NASA in 1966 and participated in the Apollo 14 mission as a lunar module pilot. As they returned to Earth, a number of experiments and lunar samples were collected. His strong interest in parapsychology led him to form the Institute of Noetic Sciences in 1973 in his retirement. Additionally, he was a co-founder of the Association of Space Explorers in 1984.

Awards and Honors: Presidential Medal of Freedom, USN Distinguished Service Medal, USAF Aerospace Research Award, etc. Congressional Space Medal of Honor, NASA Exceptional Achievement Medal, NASA Distinguished Service Medal, Distinguised Flying Cross.

David Randolph Scott (1932)

He had attended the United States Military Academy before graduating in 1954, pursuing his career as an Airforce pilot. Eventually, he later joined NASA, and in his first mission, he accompanied Neil Armstrong as the Pilot of the Gemini 8 mission (1966). And later flew again on the Apollo 9 mission (1969) as the Command Module Pilot. On July 26, 1971, he boarded the Apollo 15 flight mission as the commander. Wherein, it was the fourth crewed lunar landing, and the very first to use a Lunar Roving Vehicle (LRV).

Awards and Honors: Congressional Space Medal of Honor, NASA Exceptional Achievement Medal.

James Benson Irwin (1930-1991)

He had graduated from the U.S. Naval Academy to later serve as a fighter pilot in the U.S. Air Force. He boarded the Apollo 15 mission as the Lunar Module Pilot (1971). Irwin, along with Commander David R. Scott, spent approximately 3 days on the moon, conducting scientific experiments and investigations while exploring on a Lunar Roving Vehicle, succesfully returning with lunar rocks and core samples. The Apollo 15 mission was his sole space mission before retiring from space programs to later establish the High Flight Foundation, a Christian evangelical organization.

Awards and Honors: NASA Distinguished Service Medal, Command Pilot Astronaut Wings, etc.

John Watts Young (1930-2018)

He had joined the U.S. Navy and served as a fighter pilot before pursuing his career as a test pilot for NASA. He is recognized as first person to make six spaceflights, takin part in several space missions such as the Gemini, Apollo, And Space Shuttle programs, as follows: Gemini 3 (1964), Gemini 10 (1966), Apollo 10 (1969), Apollo 16 (1972). On his fifth space mission in 1981, he boarded as the Commander of the STS-1, the first Space Shuttle mission. Before he flew once again as Commander of STS-9, the first Spacelab mission.

Awards and Honors: Congressional Space Medal of Honor, NASA Distinguished Service Medals, etc.

Charles Moss Duke Jr. (1935)

He had graduated from the U.S. Naval Academy upon receiving a degree in Naval Sciences and his Master's in Aeronautics and Astronautics at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (1964) before he served in the U.S. Air Force. In 1966, he was selected as an astronaut by NASA, wherein, he participated in the Apollo 10 flight as a member of the astronaut support crew. Later boarded as Capsule Communicator (CAPCOM) for Apollo 11, and became backup lunar module pilot on Apollo 13. Followed by Apollo 16 wherein, he was the lunar module pilot, under Commander John W. Young.

Awards and Honors: NASA Distinguished Service Medal, JSC Certificate of Commendation, etc.

Eugene Andrew Cernan (1934-2017)

In 1956, he served in the U.S. Navy as a Naval Aviator before joining NASA in 1963, after earning his Master's in Aeronautical Engineering. His first mission had been in Gemini 9 (1966) walking along the surface of the moon in a faulty space suit. He then boarded the Apollo 10 mission (1969) as the lunar module pilot where he orbitted the Moon and tested landing procedures. Apollo 17 (1972) was the last Apollo Moon program, where he and Harrison Schmitt, explored the Taurus-Littrow valley of the moon's surface, collecting lunar samples.

Awards and Honors: NASA Distinguished Service Medals, JSC Superior Achievement Award, etc.

Harrison Haggan Schmitt (1935)

An American Geologist, Astronaut, and Politician. He received a Ph.D in Geology in 1963 before employing at the U.S. Geological Survey. He later participated in NASA's Space mission Apollo 17 (1972), where he conducted a research on lunar geology, collecting lunar samples and insights on the moon. He and Commander Eugene A. Cernan were the last men to walk on the moon, the final Apollo Moon landing mission. After his return to Earth, he departed from NASA before diving into politics, where he was succesfully elected as a U.S. Senate from New Mexico (1976).

Awards and Honors: NASA Distinguished Service Medal, NASA Distinguished Public Service Medal.


 

ANIMALS ON SPACE

Laika (1957)

A small stray dog from the streets of Moscow, Russia. She underwent various training under Soviet Scientists in preparation for her space mission. On November 3, 1957, the Soviet Sputnik Spacecraft was launched where she was distinguished as the first living being to orbit space. However, her vitals showed a spike in heart rate, which later caused her death.

Belka and Strelka (1960)

Were deemed as the first living creatures to succesfully return from space. Both Belka and Strelka were stray dogs from the streets of Mowscow, Russia. Taken under Soviet Scientists in preparation for space exploration, they boarded the Soviet Sputnik 5 alongside other animals, before returning safely on August 20, 1960. This great feat was celebrated for the conclusion living organisms can survive through space. After their return, they were given a normal life, with Strelka bearing puppies of her own.

Ham the Chimp (1957-1983)

He arrived in the United States and was selected by NASA to perform a suborbital spaceflight test and other experiments in preparation for space. In 1961, Ham, boarded the Mercury-Redstone 2 Spacecraft. He performed several tasks, monitoring his behavior and the general response of a living body towards space environment. He was successfully retrieved after his tests were concluded, collecting valuable insights and knowledge from the mission.

Félicette (1963)

A stray feline cat from the streets of France. She was among the 14 other Female cats trained for spaceflight under the French space program. She first boarded on the French Veronique AG1 rocket, traveling in a suborbital flight that lasted approximately 15 minutes. Sensors were attached on her body, monitoring her vitals and acquiring data before she safely returned to Earth. However, two months after her return, she had died from undergoing examinations of her brain.
